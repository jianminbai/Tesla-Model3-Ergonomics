# English Summary

This project is a Chinese open-source guide to Tesla Model 3 driving ergonomics, biomechanics and seat-pressure analysis.

It explains why some drivers experience sit-bone pain, posterior thigh pressure, gluteal soft-tissue compression, lower-back discomfort and right-leg fatigue during Model 3 driving. Instead of treating these symptoms as isolated seat-adjustment tips, the guide connects them through pressure distribution, pelvic posture, seat geometry, pedal control and repeatable driving experiments.

## What This Guide Covers

- How sitting transfers body weight from the legs to the pelvis, sit bones, gluteal tissue, thighs and backrest.
- Why pressure cannot disappear, but can be redistributed across better load-bearing areas.
- How pelvic posterior tilt, neutral posture and excessive anterior tilt change sit-bone and thigh loading.
- Why Model 3 seat height, cushion front edge, side bolsters, backrest angle and lumbar support interact with each other.
- Why the right leg may feel more fatigued because it combines static support with continuous accelerator control.
- How to record seat changes, symptom scores and driving duration in a small engineering experiment.

## Intended Readers

This guide is useful for:

- Tesla Model 3 drivers who feel discomfort after 20 to 90 minutes of driving.
- Drivers who want to distinguish sit-bone point pain, soft-tissue compression and posterior thigh pressure.
- Readers who want a structured seat-adjustment process instead of trial-and-error changes.
- Contributors who want to submit anonymized cases, diagrams, corrections or comparisons with Model Y and other vehicles.

## Method

The project uses a three-layer method:

1. **Biomechanics first**: pressure, contact area, pelvic posture and muscle tension.
2. **Vehicle-specific analysis**: Model 3 seat cushion, side bolsters, front edge, backrest, lumbar support and pedal geometry.
3. **Experiment records**: CSV templates, Markdown logs and simple Python analysis for repeatable observations.

## Medical Disclaimer

This project is for ergonomics education and structured self-observation. It is not medical diagnosis or treatment advice. Persistent numbness, radiating pain, weakness, night pain or symptoms that do not improve after reducing exposure should be discussed with a physician or physical therapist.
